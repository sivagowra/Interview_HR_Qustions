What is RCPA?

RCPA stands for Retail Chemist Prescription Audit. RCPA is a sales survey tool which is used to gather the competitor knowledge.

RCPA is important in the pharmaceutical industry to understand sales trends, improve marketing strategies, and grow business performance.

It also helps to meet doctors, explain your product and competitor brands like TRIMA, and understand their prescriptions.

Features of RCPA

Sales Tracking

It shows how many products are sold.

Shop Data

Information is collected from medical shops or stores.

Competitor Check

It helps to compare your products with competitor products.

Customer Demand

It shows which products customers are buying more.

Market Analysis

It helps to understand the current market situation.

Improvement

It helps companies improve sales and plan better.

Circulatory System

The circulatory system means to circulates the blood throughout the body and transports oxygen & various vital material each and every cell of the body is called circulatory system.

In the circulatory system main components there are three:

  • Heart
  • Blood vessels
  • Blood

Blood vessels: Blood vessels are tubes that carry blood throughout body. There are three types:

  • Artery (away from the heart)
  • Veins (to the heart)
  • Capillaries (tiny connectors)

Heart: The heart pumps the blood throughout the body. In heart there are four chambers:

  • Left and right atria (upper chamber)
  • Left and right ventricles (lower chamber)

Blood: The blood contains red blood cells (carry oxygen), white blood cells (fight infection), platelets (help with blood clotting), and plasma (the fluid).

  • Gives shape to our body

  • Stores minerals like calcium
  • It supports the body moves

Blood Circulation in Heart

Blood circulation in the heart is the process of how blood moves into the heart, through the heart, and out to the body.

Digestive System

The digestive system is a complex, continuous tract from the mouth to the anus, along with accessory organs (liver, pancreas, gallbladder) that breaks down food into nutrients for energy, growth, and repair. It converts food into simple forms like glucose and amino acids, absorbs them into the bloodstream, and eliminates waste.

Key Parts of the Digestive System

Gastrointestinal (GI) Tract (Hollow Organs)

  • Mouth
  • Esophagus
  • Stomach
  • Small Intestine
  • Large Intestine (including rectum)
  • Anus

Solid Organs (Accessory Organs)

  • Liver (produces bile)
  • Gallbladder (stores bile)
  • Pancreas (produces digestive enzymes)

Support Structures

  • Teeth (grind food)
  • Tongue
  • Salivary Glands

Endocrine System

The main function of your endocrine system is to release hormones into your blood while continuously monitoring the levels. Hormones deliver their messages by locking into the cells they target so they can relay the message. You have more than 50 different hormones, and they affect nearly all aspects of your health — directly or indirectly.

Functions of the Endocrine System

  • Metabolism
  • Homeostasis (constant internal balance), such as blood pressure and blood sugar regulation, fluid (water) and electrolyte balance, and body temperature
  • Growth and development
  • Sexual function
  • Reproduction
  • Sleep-wake cycle
  • Mood

Very small amounts of hormones can trigger significant responses and changes in your body. If your body has too little or too much of a hormone, it affects your health. This often causes noticeable symptoms.

Respiratory System

The respiratory body system primary function is gas exchange to taking the oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide.

In the respiratory body system main functions there are:

  • Gas exchange
  • Breathing
  • Air filtration
  • Protection
  • Sound production

The respiratory body system major organs:

  • Nose → filters, warms, moistens air
  • Throat → passage way for air and food
  • Voice box → contains vocal cords
  • Wind pipe → directs air to the lungs
  • Lungs → gas exchange

Inhalation brings oxygen into the lungs.

Exhalation expels carbon dioxide from the body.

Steps of Blood Flow

From Body to Heart

Dirty (low oxygen) blood comes from the body to the right side of the heart.

Right Atrium → Right Ventricle

Blood enters the right atrium, then moves to the right ventricle.

Heart to Lungs

The right ventricle sends blood to the lungs to get oxygen.

From Lungs to Heart

Clean (oxygen-rich) blood comes back to the left side of the heart.

Left Atrium → Left Ventricle

Blood enters the left atrium, then moves to the left ventricle.

Heart to Body

The left ventricle pumps oxygen-rich blood to the whole body.

Simple Flow (Easy to Remember)

Body → Right Heart → Lungs → Left Heart → Body

Short Definition (for exams)

Blood circulation in the heart is the process of pumping deoxygenated blood to the lungs and oxygenated blood to the body.

There are 11 Body Systems

  1. Cardiovascular System (Circulatory System): It transports blood, oxygen, nutrients, and waste throughout the body.
  2. Digestive System: This system breaks down food into nutrients and removes waste.
  3. Endocrine System: This system uses hormones to control growth, metabolism, and reproduction.
  4. Integumentary System: This includes skin, hair, and nails. It protects the body.
  5. Lymphatic System: This system helps in immunity, fluid balance, and fat absorption.
  6. Muscular System: This system helps in movement and produces heat.
  7. Nervous System: This is the body’s control center.
  8. Reproductive System: This system is responsible for producing offspring.
  9. Respiratory System: This system helps in breathing (oxygen in, carbon dioxide out).
  10. Skeletal System: This includes bones and cartilage. It gives structure and protection.
  11. Urinary System: This system removes waste from the blood as urine.
